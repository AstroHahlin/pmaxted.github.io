Benchmark stars for the PLATO mission

A compilation of eclipsing binary benchmark stars for the PLATO mission.

Using a combination of both space and ground based observations, this page compiles a list of well-studied eclipsing binaries with accurate fundamental parameters as well as high quality and high resolution spectra that can be used for further characterisation of their composition and structure.

Motivation/Background

The motiation for this project is that, as high quality data of millions stars have been collected, is to provide a database of high quality stellar spectra obtained from detached eclising binary stars where their stellar parameters have been determined with high accuracy.

Binaries are particularly interesting for this purpose. The reason for this is that their stellar parameters can be determined independently from conventional stellar models. Thanks to their orbital motion and eclipses, the masses and radii of the stellar components can be determined with high accuracy. Thanks to the recent developments in distance measurments by the GAIA project, it is also possible to obtain high precision effective temperatures for each components.

PLATO (PLAnetary Transits and Oscillations of stars) is the third medium-class mission in ESA's Cosmic Vision programme. PLATO was adopted by ESA Science Programme Committee in June 2017 and is due to be launched in 2026. Its objective is to find and study a large number of extrasolar planetary systems, with emphasis on the properties of terrestrial planets in the habitable zone around solar-like stars. PLATO has also been designed to investigate seismic activity in stars, enabling the precise characterisation of the planet host star, including its age.

Citizen scientist contributions

While much of this work relies on large astronomical telescopes, significant progress can be made using observations from citizen scientists. The reason for this is that we focus on bright targets that can readily be observed even with smaller telescopes. In addition, the relatively long (~5 days and longer) orbital periods makes scheduling on large facilities challenging.

Some work partialy dependent on citizen scientist observations have already been successfully carried out. One such example is CPD-54 810 (Please verify that this is correct) that used photometric observations from the 0.3 m Perth Exoplanet Survey Telescope (PEST).

Here, the current collection of spectra for benchmark eclipsing binary stars, i.e. stars for which we have precise and accurate mass, radius and effective temperature measurements. The goal is to continue expanding this list with the help of high quality photometric and spectroscopic observations.

Methodology

May not be nessecary, or move to a separate place with more technical details.

ellc
Fast and accurate light curve model for transiting exoplanets and eclipsing binary stars.
qpower2
A very fast and accurate algorithm for the computation of exoplanet transit light curves with the power-2 limb-darkening law.
pycheops
Python module for the analysis of light curves from the ESA CHEOPS mission.
bagemass
Bayesian mass and age estimates for transiting exoplanet host stars.

Targets of Interest

The following list contains eclipsing binary stars where scientific analysis is currently ongoing. Spectroscopic observations have been obtained with the high resolution spectrographs HARPS+NIRPS. The targets are Sun-like stars with an M dwarf companion contributing between 1 and 3 % of the total flux.

  • CD-31 3271
  • HD 4875
  • HD 287990
